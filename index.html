Spatio-Temporal District Reconstruction & Socio-Economic Nowcasting

1. The core idea

IndiSight-Nowcast is a multi-modal machine learning and geospatial analytics framework designed to address the "data-lag problem" in Indian governance. Satellite imagery is available intermittently; official socio-economic statistics (NFHS surveys, MGNREGA, PMGSY) are released with 6-12 month lags. This project fuses satellite imagery with historical tabular data to nowcast current district-level development indicators in near-real-time.

Our work: We built an end-to-end pipeline using 97,000+ NFHS records across 728 Indian districts over 7 years, generated 50+ interactive EDA artifacts, and deployed a Streamlit dashboard with spatio-temporal visualizations and an LLM agent for policy-guided interpretation.

2. Data-Lag is the Problem:

2.1 Challenge

India's development monitoring relies on periodic surveys (NFHS-5 conducted 2019-2021; previous round 2015-2016). Real-time satellite imagery is abundant but intermittent due to cloud cover. This creates a critical gap:

  • Policy makers need current development metrics to allocate resources (roads via PMGSY, employment via MGNREGA).
  • Satellite data reflects physical infrastructure (electrification, sanitation facilities) in near-real-time.
  • Missing link: Can we use satellite observations to infer current socio-economic status between survey rounds?

2.2 Our Approach

We designed a spatio-temporal nowcasting framework that learns mappings from:

  • Satellite embeddings (Prithvi-100M Vision Transformer) → multi-spectral district signatures
  • Tabular features (PMGSY road investment, MGNREGA employment demand) → district infrastructure intensity
  • Historical NFHS data → ground-truth socio-economic outcomes

The system learns to predict current district-level development metrics (e.g., electrification, sanitation, education) by fusing these three signal sources.

3. Methodology & Implementation

3.1 Data Sources & Preparation

  • NFHS-4/5 (2015-2021): 97,386 harmonized metric records across 728 districts and 82 health indicators.
  • PMGSY (2015-2019): Road length and sanction cost by district—proxy for infrastructure investment.
  • MGNREGA (2015-2019): Employment demand and labor expenditure—indicator of rural economic stress.
  • District Boundaries (LGD codes): 728 Indian districts with consistent coding across datasets.
  • Satellite Imagery (GEE): Sentinel-2 time-series (6 bands: Blue, Green, Red, NIR, SWIR-1, SWIR-2).
Data Harmonization Challenge: NFHS CSVs contain 150+ cryptic column names with unit suffixes. We built an explicit mapping table to standardize metrics (e.g., "women_age_group_15_to_49_years_who_are_literate" → "women_literate").

The district maps show clear regional clustering (hotspots and coldspots), consistent with the Moran’s I results. This is critical for IndiSight-Nowcast: when the ground-truth outcomes cluster geographically, satellite imagery becomes a powerful proxy because adjacent districts share similar built form, land use, and infrastructure.

Electrification (2019, spatial map). Most districts are near saturation (values clustered close to 100%), with a small set of lagging pockets. For modeling, this implies a ceiling effect: predicting the remaining low-coverage tail is more informative than predicting already-saturated districts.

Electrification (2019, top/bottom states). State rankings compress near the upper bound, reinforcing that electrification is largely achieved in many states. The remaining variance is concentrated in a smaller set of states, guiding where targeted monitoring/nowcasting is highest value.

Child Stunting (2019, spatial map). Unlike electrification, stunting remains widely dispersed with clear regional hotspots. This “health-outcome surface” is exactly the kind of signal we want to nowcast between survey rounds: it changes over time, shows geographic structure, and links to both infrastructure and socio-economic context.

Child Stunting (2019, top/bottom states; lower is better). The spread between best and worst states is large, indicating substantial health inequality. This helps prioritize where a nowcasting model is most policy-relevant (high-stunting states with many vulnerable districts).

4.5 Infrastructure-Outcome Correlations: What Explains What?

Scatter plots relate “inputs” (PMGSY roads; MGNREGA demand/expenditure) to “outcomes” (NFHS indicators). The most important interpretation is not just correlation strength, but why relationships look weak: saturation (ceiling effects), confounding (roads are built more in hard-to-reach districts), and granularity mismatch (state-level program variables paired with district-level outcomes).

PMGSY vs electrification. The apparent relationship is limited by a ceiling effect: once electrification is near 100%, additional road length cannot “increase” an already saturated metric. Modeling-wise, this motivates focusing on the low-electrification tail rather than average trends.

Road length vs improved sanitation. Wide spread suggests roads alone are insufficient to predict sanitation. This supports the project design choice to use multi-modal features (satellite + multiple tabular signals) rather than relying on a single program variable.

4.6 Data Completeness: Which Metrics Are Reliable to Model?

NFHS contains many indicators with substantial missingness, especially those introduced in later survey rounds or those requiring detailed behavioral recall. We therefore focus modeling and interpretability on a core set of high-coverage indicators (electricity, sanitation, clean cooking fuel, institutional births, women’s schooling, child stunting, women’s anemia).

Top 30 metrics by missingness. The most missing variables are often meta-fields or highly specific behavioral questions (e.g., feeding practice details). For robust nowcasting, we prioritize indicators with low missingness and clear measurement definitions.

5. Satellite Imagery & Vision Model Reconstruction

5.1 Foundation Model Approach: Prithvi-100M Vision Transformer

Beyond tabular features and traditional remote sensing indices, we deployed Prithvi-100M, a Vision Transformer (ViT) pre-trained on 100 million satellite images from the IBM-NASA GeoAI Foundation Model project. This model learns to extract meaningful spatial and spectral patterns from multi-spectral satellite data—patterns that correlate strongly with human development indicators.

Why Prithvi? Unlike traditional indices (NDVI, EVI) that use hand-crafted band ratios, foundation models learn rich embeddings from raw spectral data. For nowcasting, these embeddings capture: built-up density, vegetation vigor, water bodies, field patterns, and infrastructure—all proxies for development status without explicit coding.

5.2 How We Obtained These Images

  • Data Source: Sentinel-2 Level 2A surface reflectance (10m resolution, 6 bands: Blue, Green, Red, NIR, SWIR-1, SWIR-2) from Google Earth Engine.
  • Coverage: Indian districts, aggregated to district-level composites (2019 wet season to capture vegetation and water bodies).
  • Processing:
    1. Download Sentinel-2 time-series for each district (2019, cloud-free months)
    2. Composite to 30m × 30m grid (downsampled for computational efficiency)
    3. Feed to Prithvi-100M encoder → extract 768-dimensional embeddings per district patch
    4. Spatially aggregate embeddings across district to single district-level vector
    5. Reconstruct surface reflectance from embeddings using decoder head (foundation model inference)

5.3 Model Reconstruction Intensity Map

The first image shows reconstruction intensity across all 728 districts. This is a pixelated heatmap where each cell represents a district's "reconstruction plausibility"—how well the Prithvi model can recreate the satellite data from learned embeddings.

Figure 5.1: Prithvi-100M Reconstruction Intensity (All 728 Districts). Intensity ranges 0.0–1.4, indicating model confidence in reconstructed satellite patterns. Dark blue patches (low intensity ~0.0–0.2): Districts with sparse vegetation or unique spectral signatures (e.g., desert, alpine regions) where foundation model has fewer learned priors. Green-yellow patches (high intensity ~0.8–1.0): Districts with common patterns (agricultural fields, urban sprawl) that the model can reconstruct accurately. Interpretation: This validates that development-correlated landscapes (dense settlements, irrigation) are well-learned by the foundation model—a signal that embeddings will be meaningful for nowcasting.

5.4 Ground Truth vs. Reconstruction: NIR Band Fidelity

The second image compares original Sentinel-2 NIR band (ground truth) against Prithvi's reconstructed NIR band. This is a direct test of reconstruction fidelity: can the model accurately recreate spectral information from its learned embeddings?

Figure 5.2: NIR Band Ground Truth vs. Prithvi Reconstruction. Left (Ground Truth): Raw Sentinel-2 NIR reflectance (0–4000 physical units). Shows natural heterogeneity: dark patches = water/urban, bright patches = healthy vegetation, medium patches = mixed agricultural/built-up. Right (Reconstruction): NIR band reconstructed from Prithvi embeddings. Shows smoother patterns with preserved district-level structure but reduced local detail. Interpretation: The reconstruction is not pixel-perfect (nor needs to be). What matters: (1) District-level structure is preserved (agriculture vs. urban clusters are visible in both), (2) Spectral ranges are realistic (no negative values or extreme outliers), (3) Smoothness indicates embeddings capture coarse patterns—ideal for district-level nowcasting where we don't need sub-meter precision.

5.5 What This Means for Nowcasting

Validation of the Embedding Strategy: The reconstruction shows that Prithvi embeddings capture meaningful satellite signatures. Similar-looking embeddings → similar reconstructed spectra → similar underlying landscape development status.
Proof of Concept: If embeddings can faithfully encode spectral patterns, they can encode development correlates (built-up density, vegetation health, irrigation extent). These correlates are precisely what we need to predict NFHS outcomes.
Next Step: The embeddings will feed into supervised models (XGBoost, neural networks) trained on 2015–2019 NFHS + tabular features. Once trained, new satellite data → embeddings → nowcast predictions for current (2024–2026) development metrics without waiting for NFHS-6 survey.

5.6 Some notes

  • The reconstructed NIR is smoother than ground truth because embeddings compress fine spatial detail into lower-dimensional vectors. This is not a flaw—it's a feature. Nowcasting doesn't require pixel-level reconstruction; we need district-level patterns.
  • Reconstruction intensity is a composite metric combining per-band MSE, SSIM (structural similarity), and confidence. Values >1.0 indicate the model is confident in the reconstruction; values <0.5 suggest rare or out-of-distribution spectra.
  • 2019 composites emphasize the wet season (July–Sept). This biases embeddings toward vegetation vigor. For true year-round nowcasting, multi-season composites (winter dry season + summer monsoon) would be ideal.
  • We downsampled to 30m to fit computational constraints. Finer resolution (10m) would improve detail but require a larger model or GPU cluster.

6. Conclusions & Policy Recommendations

6.1 Key Takeaways

1. Spatial Clustering is Strong. Moran's I > 0.6 for most metrics indicates geography-driven outcomes. This validates nowcasting: satellite imagery (spatial) is a strong proxy for development status.
2. Progress is Uneven. National trends show improvement, but distributions widen—disparities growing. Some districts left behind despite national gains.
3. Infrastructure Correlates Selectively. Roads correlate with electrification and healthcare access but NOT with education or nutrition. Multi-sector interventions needed, not one-size-fits-all infrastructure push.
4. MGNREGA Reveals Economic Stress. High employment demand clusters in disadvantaged districts—confirms MGNREGA's intended targeting but shows persistence of rural poverty.
5. Nowcasting is Feasible. High spatial autocorrelation + multi-source data enables learning embeddings-to-outcomes mappings. Similar-looking satellites → similar development profiles.

6.2 Actionable Policy Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Targeted Healthcare Infrastructure. Districts with high stunting but low institutional births need healthcare workers and facilities, not just roads. Water + sanitation + healthcare frontline staff are bottlenecks.
Recommendation 2: Education-First in Lagging Regions. Women's education shows highest spatial clustering. Investing in girls' education in Bihar, Jharkhand, NE could unlock cascading development improvements.
Recommendation 3: Nationwide Nutrition Supplementation. Anemia persists everywhere with weak geographic clustering and worsened between 2015 and 2019. This suggests a need for health- and diet-specific interventions (supplementation, infection control, maternal health services) alongside infrastructure improvements.
Recommendation 4: Deploy Quarterly Nowcasting. Once trained, run model on new satellite data quarterly to track district-level progress without waiting for NFHS survey cycles. Enables rapid policy response.
